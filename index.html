Ready
Is it possible to capture clear photographs of bullets as they travel through the air, even if they're moving faster than the speed of sound? What about photographs of objects being penetrated by bullets at the time of the impact? Here we discuss the concepts behind photographing this subject as well as the necessary materials for doing so.

The single most important idea behind taking these crisp photos is that the exposure time must be very short. This is because the bullet will be moving at a very high speed, so we require that the flash be short enough to minimize motion blur. In this example, we use a .22 caliber rifle with 25.4mm long bullets, which travel at approximately 340m/s. This means that to get an unnoticeable motion blur, we want the subject to move just around .1% of the subject's length. Therefore, we need to capture it before it can travel a distance of .0254mm. The time it takes for this to happen is just (.0254mm)/(340m/s) = .0747ms. Finding a strobe that will fire for just this short amount of time will allow us to photograph a moving bullet.

With this information in mind, we know that we can get a good photograph if we use some short enough exposure time. There are two ways to get this - either by having the shutter open for just .0747ms, or by using a light source that lasts just .0747ms. In this experiment, we will work with a strobe with a flash just under .0747ms.

Fire
The procedure for taking these photos is outlined below:
  1. 1.

    Setup the Subject. Begin by ensuring that the subject is in the correct position. In order to take the photo of the crayon, we propped the crayon up with some clay along the line of fire of the rifle. To take the photo of the playing card, we inserted it into one of the ridges of the chalkboard eraser so it would stand up straight.

  2. 2.

    Setup the Rifle. Secure the rifle in the appropriate position by looking through the sights. If applicable, use the halogen lamp to light the edge of the card for better accuracy. Make sure that the appropriate edge of the subject is bisected by the sights and is not twisted in any direction.

  3. 3.

    Setup the Camera. Adjust the camera such that it is perpendicular to the line of fire from the rifle. Make sure to frame the subject such that there is ample space for the subject, any interesting debris, and the bullet.

  4. 4.

    Setup the Strobe. Position the strobe such that the hotspot from the strobe hits the subject as desired. Take a couple shots of the subject with the strobe to get the best exposure. Make sure to place the sensor and set the delay with appropriate numbers to catch the bullet in the desired position.

  5. 5.

    Take Photos.

    1. Once everything is set up properly, ensure everyone is in a safe location.
    2. Then, load the bullet into the gun.
    3. Check that eye and ear protection are properly worn.
    4. Next, turn off the lights.
    5. Open the shutter to the camera.
    6. Count down "3, 2, 1, fire" and fire the rifle.
    7. Close the shutter.
    8. Turn on the lights.
    9. Replace the subject as necessary.

  6. 6.

    Clean Up. Put everything away in its proper place.
